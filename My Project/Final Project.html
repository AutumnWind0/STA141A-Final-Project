Predicting Visual Stimulus Experiment Outcomes Using Neural Spike Train Data¶

STA 141 Final Project by Yingchun Yuan 919552555¶

Abstract¶

This project examines the use of neural spike train data to predict the outcomes of visual stimulus experiments in mice. The analysis is based on data collected from 18 sessions involving four mice. Our core question is how to predict trial outcomes from these spike trains, given the complexity of high dimensional neuron data spanning multiple brain areas.
Our logistic regression model achieved a test accuracy of 74.5% and an AUC of 0.7195 on the dedicated test data from Sessions 1 and 18. These findings highlight the importance of both neural firing rates and stimulus contrasts in shaping trial outcomes, demonstrating that multi-session neural recordings can be effectively integrated to predict behavioral performance in a visual discrimination task.

Session 1: Introduction¶

The focus of this project is to develop a predictive model that uses neural spike train data and visual stimulus contrast information to forecast behavioral outcomes in mice.

The dataset, originating from experiments on four mice over 18 sessions, includes detailed records of trial feedback, stimulus contrasts, and time-resolved spike counts from various brain areas. An initial exploratory analysis identifies important data features and potential patterns, while a data integration step consolidates trial-level information across sessions. Finally, a logistic regression model is applied to predict trial outcomes, taking into account both stimulus conditions and the summarized neural activity. This approach aims to provide a clear demonstration of effective data processing and predictive modeling techniques in a practical project setting.

This project examines neural activity data from 18 sessions collected from four mice—Cori, Forssmann, Hench, and Lederberg. Each session includes the following important variables:

  • Contrast: The contrast levels of the stimulus presented on either the left or right side. This determines the sensory input the mice receives.
  • Feedback: Indicates whether the trial was successful (1) or a failure (-1), providing a measure of trial performance.
  • Time: Represents the time bin centers used to segment neural spike activity.
  • Spikes: The recorded spike counts of neurons within the visual cortex, measured across predefined time bins.
  • Brain Area: Identifies the specific region of the brain where each recorded neuron is located, allowing for spatial analysis of neural activity.

We analyze data integration steps that account for session-to-session differences and highlight the significance of contrast-based stimuli in shaping behavior.

By focusing on shared features, we aim to build a model that generalizes well across different mice and experimental sessions, ultimately connecting neural signatures to decision-making processes.

Session 3: Data Integration¶

To integrate the heterogeneous neural data collected across 18 sessions, we adopted a feature-level integration strategy based on common brain areas. Rather than focusing on the difference between left and right contrasts, we retain the individual stimulus contrast values as independent predictors. For each trial, we computed the average spike count within each recorded brain area and merged these with trial metadata (including contrast_left, contrast_right, feedback type, and overall neural activity). This approach standardizes the data across sessions by focusing on shared neural features and mitigates session-specific variability.

Additional Feature Engineering¶

We further enhanced the integrated dataset by adding new features such as the contrast difference and a decision variable. The decision variable categorizes trials based on whether the left or right contrast was dominant, or if there was no stimulus. These additional features enrich the dataset and are expected to improve the predictive model’s performance.

Session 4: Predictive Modeling¶

Model Development¶

We developed a logistic regression model using the integrated dataset derived from the training sessions. The predictors include:

  • contrast_right: Estimate = -1.249599, p < 0.001, indicating that higher right stimulus contrast is associated with lower odds of success.
  • avg_spks: Estimate = 3.053185, p < 0.001, suggesting that increased overall neural activity significantly increases the likelihood of success.
  • contrast_diff: Estimate = 1.260918, p < 0.001, which implies that larger contrast differences (i.e., greater stimulus disparity) are linked to higher trial success.

These coefficients suggest that both stimulus parameters and neural activity patterns—particularly in specific brain regions (e.g., brain_area_CA1, brain_area_BLA, brain_area_LD, brain_area_GPe, and brain_area_RSP)—play critical roles in predicting behavioral outcomes.

Model Interpretation¶

The reason we focused on logistic regression is its interpretability and ease of implementation. The logistic regression model, with an AIC of 4540.9, indicates a reasonable fit given the complexity of the data. Notably, the positive association of avg_spks and contrast_diff with trial success underscores the importance of neural firing rates and stimulus disparity in driving behavior. Conversely, the negative coefficient for contrast_right may reflect a lateralized effect or specific task demands.

Session 6: Discussion¶

In this project, we investigated the potential of using neural spike train data collected over 18 sessions to predict behavioral outcomes in a visual discrimination task in mice. Our approach involved three key steps: an exploratory analysis to characterize session-level variability, a feature-level data integration method based on brain area averages (while retaining individual stimulus conditions), and the development of a logistic regression model for prediction.

Key Findings¶

  • Stimulus and Neural Activity Contributions:
    Our model indicates that both the individual stimulus contrast values and overall neural activity significantly contribute to predicting trial outcomes. In particular, higher overall neural activity and specific brain area features (e.g., from CA1, BLA, LD, GPe, and RSP) are associated with increased odds of trial success.
  • Data Integration Strategy:
    We standardized the heterogeneous data across sessions by computing brain area–level features for each trial. Notably, we retained the left and right contrast values as independent predictors rather than focusing on their difference, which aligns better with the experimental design.
  • Model Performance on Test Data:
    Final evaluation on the dedicated test sets yielded a test accuracy of 74.5% and an AUC of 0.7195. Although the model shows high specificity (94.48%), its low sensitivity (21.82%) indicates that it under-predicts successful trials. This imbalance, as reflected by a balanced accuracy of 58.15% and a Kappa of 0.2031, suggests that further model calibration, like class-balancing techniques or more advanced methods, is warranted.

Limitations & Future Directions:¶

  • Test Data Generalization:

Although the current model shows moderate performance on the test sets, the observed sensitivity–specificity imbalance indicates room for improvement.

  • Heterogeneity in Neural Recordings:

The variability in neuron counts and brain area coverage across sessions remains a challenge. Future work could explore more advanced data integration methods or incorporate additional behavioral and temporal features to better capture the underlying neural dynamics.

In summary, our study demonstrates that a feature-level integration strategy based on brain area activity can effectively consolidate multi-session neural data for behavioral prediction. While the model achieves a reasonable level of overall accuracy, it can be refined to balance sensitivity and specificity. Future work should focus on addressing these challenges to enhance the predictive power of neural decoding approaches.
